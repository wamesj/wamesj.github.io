Reforms in Education: failure paved with good intentions

The Cultural Revolution, which started from 1955 to 1976, opened up a chaotic history in modern China. Chairmen Mao, who believed the current Communist leaders were destroying the party, accused the party and its leaders, like Liushaoqi and Dingxiaoping, of having elitism, capitalist tendencies, inefficiency, bureaucratism, and loss of revolutionary fervor. He then called on the nation's young people to exterminate Chinese society's corrupted system. Mao wanted to restore the revolutionary spirit that had led to triumph in the civil war against the Kuomintang 20 years earlier.

The Red Guards, organized with millions of teenagers, believed in Mao's words: "Bombard the headquarters!" These young people broke into factories, government officials, and schools to beat managers, governors, and teachers. Quoting from Mao: "Destroy toxic weeds!", the Red guards humiliated capitalists and intellectuals publicly and sent them to countrysides for reeducation. The Red Guards also attacked libraries and museums, destroying many valuable books and cultural relics. Factories shut down led to the decline of production; school closed because students were busy "destroying the four olds"——the old philosophy, the old culture, the old custom, and the old habit. Mountains of paintings, literature, music, plays were burned and banned from viewing. This turned into an anti-cultural revolution.
